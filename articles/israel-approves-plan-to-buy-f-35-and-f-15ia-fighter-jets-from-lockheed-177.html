Israel Approves Plan to Buy F-35 and F-15IA Fighter Jets from Lockheed and Boeing

By Sarah Mitchell Senior Editor
Published: May 06, 2026 Updated: May 06, 2026 8 Min Read

Israel's defense cabinet has approved a new procurement plan to expand its fleet of F-35I Adir stealth fighters from Lockheed Martin and purchase the new F-15IA — a customized variant of Boeing's F-15EX Eagle II built specifically for the Israeli Air Force. The deal, which requires US Congressional notification and approval under the Arms Export Control Act, represents one of the largest Israeli defense acquisitions in recent years and comes at a moment of acute regional tension centered on Iran's nuclear program and its proxy forces.

Israel already operates the F-35I Adir and is the only country outside the United States to have used it in combat. The F-15IA is a newer acquisition — Israel signed its initial F-15IA Letter of Offer and Acceptance in 2019, with deliveries expected to begin in the mid-2020s. The newly approved plan expands the total numbers across both programs.

What the Deal Includes — Jets, Numbers, and Specifications

Israel's approved procurement plan covers additional F-35I Adir aircraft from Lockheed Martin and the F-15IA from Boeing. Israel has previously operated three F-35 squadrons and has publicly discussed the goal of fielding a fourth. The F-15IA order, initially for 25 aircraft with an option for more, expands Israel's heavy strike capacity beyond what the F-35's smaller payload allows.

The F-35I Adir is Israel's designation for a modified F-35A variant. It incorporates Israeli-developed electronic warfare systems, modified software to support Israeli-manufactured weapons including the Rampage supersonic air-to-ground missile, and indigenous avionics components. This level of customization is unique among F-35 operators and gives Israel capabilities not available to other countries flying the same airframe.

The F-15IA is built on the F-15EX platform — Boeing's latest and most advanced Eagle variant — and configured from the outset to integrate Israeli radar warning receivers, electronic warfare suites, and weapons systems. The F-15IA carries a significantly larger payload than the F-35, with up to 29,500 pounds of ordnance across 23 hardpoints, making it better suited for heavy strike missions that require large bunker-buster munitions or multiple precision weapons simultaneously.

Why Israel Is Buying These Aircraft Now

The timing of the approval is directly tied to Iran. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz following the US-Iran conflict that began in late February 2026 has accelerated Israeli defense planning. Iran's nuclear program has advanced significantly since 2018, with enrichment now at 60 percent purity — technically capable of being further enriched to weapons-grade in a matter of weeks. Israel's military planners have publicly stated that a credible military option against Iranian nuclear facilities requires a larger and more capable strike package than Israel currently fields.

The F-35I provides the stealth necessary to penetrate Iran's Russian-supplied S-300 air defense systems without detection. But stealth comes with a payload penalty — the F-35's internal weapons bay limits it to approximately two large munitions in stealth configuration. Destroying hardened and deeply buried facilities like Iran's Fordow enrichment plant — built inside a mountain — requires the GBU-28 Deep Throat or the Massive Ordnance Penetrator class of weapons. These are too large for the F-35's internal bay. The F-15IA, carrying these weapons externally, would operate alongside F-35Is that suppress enemy air defenses.

Beyond Iran, Israel faces active threats from Hezbollah in Lebanon — which now possesses over 150,000 rockets and precision-guided missiles — and from Houthi ballistic missile and drone attacks from Yemen. Maintaining a larger combat-ready air fleet increases Israel's ability to conduct sustained multi-front operations if required.

F-35I vs F-15IA: Two Aircraft, Two Different Roles

The F-35I and F-15IA serve complementary rather than competing roles in the Israeli Air Force, which is why Israel has chosen to expand both simultaneously rather than concentrate on one platform.

Feature F-35I Adir F-15IA
Primary Role Stealth strike, SEAD, ISR Heavy strike, air superiority
Payload Capacity ~5,700 lbs internal (stealth mode) ~29,500 lbs across 23 hardpoints
Stealth All-aspect low observable None — conventional radar signature
Top Speed Mach 1.6 Mach 2.5+
Combat Radius ~670 miles ~1,200 miles
Unit Cost (approx) ~$80M ~$100M (with Israeli systems)

In a strike on Iran, the operational concept would likely involve F-35Is penetrating ahead to suppress or destroy radar and air defense systems using stealth and standoff weapons, creating corridors through which F-15IAs — carrying heavier munitions — can reach hardened targets. This is the same suppression of enemy air defenses doctrine Israel has refined over decades, now applied at significantly greater range and against more sophisticated defenses.

Key Takeaways

  • Israel approved expanded procurement of F-35I Adir fighters from Lockheed Martin and the new F-15IA from Boeing — two complementary platforms serving stealth penetration and heavy strike roles respectively.
  • The timing is driven by Iran's advanced nuclear program, the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, and the need to sustain multi-front operations against Hezbollah, Houthi forces, and Iranian proxies simultaneously.
  • The F-15IA's 29,500-pound payload capacity allows it to carry bunker-buster munitions too large for the F-35's internal bay, making the combination of both aircraft necessary for any credible strike on hardened Iranian facilities.

Cost of the Deal and the US Approval Process

The combined value of Israel's expanded F-35 and F-15IA programs is estimated at over $10 billion across both acquisition and long-term support contracts, though Israel has not officially disclosed final contract values. F-35 unit costs for foreign military sales are approximately $80 million per aircraft excluding weapons, training, and logistics packages. F-15IA unit costs are estimated at around $100 million given the extensive Israeli-specific customization required.

A portion of Israel's aircraft purchases is funded through US Foreign Military Financing — an annual grant that provides Israel approximately $3.8 billion per year in military aid, the largest US military assistance package in the world. Aircraft procurement of this scale routinely draws on that funding stream, meaning US taxpayers partially subsidize the acquisition.

The US approval process for arms sales of this size involves a formal Letter of Request from Israel, a review by the Defense Security Cooperation Agency, and Congressional notification — typically a 30-day window during which Congress can block the sale. Given Israel's bipartisan political support in Washington, Congressional opposition to this specific sale is not expected, though the broader context of the Gaza conflict and humanitarian concerns have made some arms transfers to Israel politically sensitive in recent months.

How This Affects the Regional Military Balance

Israel's expanded F-35 and F-15IA fleet significantly increases its capacity to conduct sustained, large-scale air operations — not just single strike missions. Military analysts note that one of the limiting factors in any hypothetical Israeli strike on Iran has been sortie generation: how many aircraft Israel could put over targets at once and sustain over multiple days. The expanded fleet directly addresses that constraint.

For Iran, the expansion of Israel's stealth fleet is particularly concerning. Iran's integrated air defense network, built around Russian-supplied S-300 systems and domestic radar infrastructure, is designed to detect and engage non-stealth aircraft. F-35Is operating in stealth configuration present a fundamentally different threat that the S-300 system has limited historical effectiveness against.

Regional states including Saudi Arabia and the UAE are watching this procurement closely. Both countries have sought advanced F-35 variants as part of their own modernization programs, and Israel's expanded fleet increases pressure on Washington to decide whether and when to approve F-35 sales to Gulf states — a politically complex question given Israeli objections to sharing advanced US technology with Arab states under the longstanding US policy of maintaining Israel's qualitative military edge.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the main advantages of the F-35 fighter jet for Israel?

The F-35I Adir gives Israel all-aspect stealth capable of defeating most radar-guided air defense systems, plus sensor fusion that combines data from multiple onboard sensors into a single tactical display. Israel's version carries indigenous electronic warfare systems and is cleared for Israeli-manufactured weapons including the Rampage supersonic strike missile. It is the only aircraft Israel operates that can penetrate advanced integrated air defense systems like the S-300 without detection.

How does the F-15IA differ from the standard F-15 Eagle?

The F-15IA is built on Boeing's F-15EX Eagle II — the newest and most capable Eagle variant — and customized extensively for Israel. It integrates Israeli radar warning receivers, electronic warfare suites, and cockpit systems, and is cleared to carry Israel's full range of domestically developed weapons. Its 29,500-pound payload capacity across 23 hardpoints is roughly five times what the F-35 can carry in stealth configuration, making it the preferred platform for missions requiring heavy munitions.

How much does the Israel F-35 and F-15IA deal cost?

The combined estimated value exceeds $10 billion across both programs when including aircraft, weapons, training, and long-term support contracts. F-35 unit costs are approximately $80 million per aircraft and F-15IA units approximately $100 million given the custom Israeli configuration. A portion is funded through the $3.8 billion annual US Foreign Military Financing grant Israel receives.

What is the significance of US approval for the sale of these jets to Israel?

US approval under the Arms Export Control Act reflects the legal commitment to maintaining Israel's qualitative military edge — a policy enshrined in US law. Congressional notification is required for sales of this scale. Approval also sends a deterrence signal to Iran and other regional actors about US commitment to Israel's defense capability, particularly relevant during ongoing nuclear negotiations and the Strait of Hormuz crisis.

How will Israel's new F-35 and F-15IA jets affect the regional military balance?

The expanded fleet increases Israel's sortie generation capacity for sustained large-scale operations, directly addressing one of the limiting factors in any credible strike on distant hardened targets. Iran's S-300 air defense network is poorly positioned to counter expanded stealth operations. Saudi Arabia and the UAE will likely accelerate their own F-35 acquisition discussions in response, putting Washington in a difficult position regarding its qualitative military edge policy for Israel.

Conclusion

Israel's approved procurement of additional F-35I fighters and new F-15IA aircraft is a direct response to a deteriorating regional security environment centered on Iran's nuclear advances and the multi-front threat from Iranian proxy forces. The two aircraft serve complementary roles — the F-35I provides the stealth penetration capability to survive modern air defenses, while the F-15IA provides the payload capacity to destroy hardened targets that require heavy munitions.

The deal reflects both the depth of the US-Israel defense relationship and the limits of diplomacy in the current Middle East environment. Military procurement of this scale takes years to deliver, and Israel's approval now signals a long-term strategic assessment that the threat environment will remain severe for the foreseeable future — regardless of how current negotiations with Iran ultimately conclude.
