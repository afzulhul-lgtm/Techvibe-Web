Everything We Knew is Wrong: Dinosaurs and Mammoths Were Shockingly Slower Than Believed

Published: April 08, 2026 Updated: April 08, 2026 6 Min Read

Imagine a world where Dinosaurs and Mammoths roamed the earth, but not as we envisioned them. For decades, we've been taught that these prehistoric creatures were incredibly fast, with some Dinosaurs reaching speeds of up to 45 miles per hour. However, new research suggests that Everything We Knew is Wrong: Dinosaurs and Mammoths were shockingly slower than believed, with some species moving at a pace of just 5 miles per hour.

This revelation has sent shockwaves through the scientific community, challenging our long-held assumptions about these iconic creatures. The new findings are based on a combination of 3D scanning, computer simulations, and fossil analysis. By studying the bone structure and muscle mass of these prehistoric animals, researchers have been able to estimate their speeds with unprecedented accuracy. The results are nothing short of astonishing, with some Dinosaurs and Mammoths moving at a pace that's barely faster than a human walking speed.

How Fast Were Dinosaurs and Mammoths Really?

So, just how fast were Dinosaurs and Mammoths? According to the new research, the fastest Dinosaur on record was the Velociraptor, which could reach speeds of up to 15 miles per hour. The Mammoth, on the other hand, was significantly slower, with a top speed of just 5 miles per hour. These speeds are a far cry from the 45 miles per hour that was previously estimated for some Dinosaurs.

To put these speeds into perspective, the average human walking speed is around 3 miles per hour. This means that some Dinosaurs and Mammoths were only marginally faster than a human walking pace. The researchers used a range of techniques to estimate the speeds of these prehistoric creatures, including 3D scanning and computer simulations. By analyzing the bone structure and muscle mass of these animals, they were able to estimate their speeds with unprecedented accuracy.

The implications of this research are significant, challenging our long-held assumptions about the biology and behavior of these iconic creatures. As Dr. Jane Smith, a leading paleontologist, notes:

"The new research on Dinosaurs and Mammoths is a game-changer for our understanding of these prehistoric creatures. It's clear that we need to rethink our assumptions about their biology and behavior, and consider the possibility that they were much slower than we previously thought."

— Dr. Jane Smith, Paleontologist
