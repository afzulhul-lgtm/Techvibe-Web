Everything We Knew is Wrong: Dinosaurs and Mammoths Were Shockingly Slower Than Believed

By Dr. Amara Singh Senior Editor
Published: May 06, 2026 Updated: May 06, 2026 8 Min Read

The speed of dinosaurs and mammoths has long been a topic of fascination, with many assuming these prehistoric creatures were capable of rapid movements. However, recent research suggests that our previous estimates were incorrect, and these animals were actually much slower than we thought. The hadrosaur, a duck-billed dinosaur, and the woolly mammoth, a relative of the modern elephant, are two examples of creatures that have been reevaluated in terms of their speed.

Understanding the speed of these prehistoric animals is crucial for reconstructing their behavior, habitat, and interactions with other species. For instance, the hadrosaur's speed would have affected its ability to escape predators like the tyrannosaur, while the woolly mammoth's speed would have influenced its migration patterns during the Pleistocene epoch. The discovery that these creatures were slower than previously thought has significant implications for our understanding of their biology and ecology.

Studies of dinosaur tracks and skeletal anatomy have led researchers to reestimate the speed of these creatures. The hadrosaur, for example, was previously thought to be capable of speeds up to 45 kilometers per hour. However, more recent estimates suggest that it likely moved at a pace of around 10-15 kilometers per hour. Similarly, the woolly mammoth was believed to be able to run at speeds of up to 40 kilometers per hour, but new research indicates that it probably moved at a slower pace of around 5-10 kilometers per hour.

These estimates are based on detailed analyses of fossilized tracks and the anatomy of the animals' legs and skeletons. For instance, the presence of powerful leg muscles and specialized foot structures in the hadrosaur suggests that it was well-adapted for walking and running. However, the relatively short length of its legs and the presence of heavy body armor indicate that it was not built for high speeds.

The discovery of well-preserved hadrosaur and mammoth tracks in the Hell Creek Formation, a geological formation in Montana, has provided valuable insights into the locomotion patterns of these animals. The tracks suggest that these creatures moved in a slow and deliberate manner, with the hadrosaur taking around 2-3 seconds to complete a single stride.

Why Our Previous Estimates Were Wrong

Our previous estimates of dinosaur and mammoth speed were based on a combination of factors, including their body size, leg length, and muscle power. However, these estimates were often simplistic and did not take into account the complexities of animal locomotion. For example, the hadrosaur's powerful leg muscles and specialized foot structures were thought to indicate high speeds, but they may have been adapted for other functions, such as support and balance.

Additionally, our previous estimates were often influenced by comparisons with modern animals, such as horses and elephants. However, these comparisons are not always valid, as the anatomy and physiology of prehistoric creatures were often very different from those of their modern counterparts. The hadrosaur, for instance, had a unique pelvic structure and a distinctive pattern of weight distribution, which would have affected its locomotion patterns.

How Researchers Measured Their Speed

Researchers used a variety of methods to estimate the speed of dinosaurs and mammoths, including the analysis of fossilized tracks, skeletal anatomy, and biomechanical modeling. The study of fossilized tracks, for example, can provide valuable information about an animal's stride length, stride frequency, and overall locomotion pattern. By analyzing the tracks and using mathematical models, researchers can estimate the animal's speed and energy expenditure.

Biomechanical modeling involves the use of computer simulations to model the movement of an animal's limbs and body. This approach can provide detailed information about an animal's locomotion patterns and can help researchers to estimate its speed and energy expenditure. For example, a study on the hadrosaur used biomechanical modeling to estimate its speed and found that it was likely much slower than previously thought.

The use of 3D scanning and printing technology has also revolutionized the field of paleontology, allowing researchers to create highly detailed models of fossilized tracks and skeletons. These models can be used to analyze the anatomy and locomotion patterns of prehistoric creatures in unprecedented detail, providing new insights into their biology and behavior.
