Everything We Knew is Wrong: Dinosaurs and Mammoths Were Shockingly Slower Than Believed

By {{AUTHOR_NAME}} Senior Editor
Published: May 08, 2026 Updated: May 08, 2026 5 Min Read

A recent study published in the journal PLOS ONE has dramatically revised our understanding of the running speeds of dinosaurs and mammoths, with findings that challenge decades of popular science presentations and Hollywood portrayals. The research used advanced computer modeling, muscle mass reconstruction, and locomotion physics to estimate the realistic locomotion capabilities of species including Tyrannosaurus rex, Triceratops, and the woolly mammoth.

The study's results are particularly significant because they reshape our understanding of prehistoric predator-prey dynamics, suggesting that Tyrannosaurus rex may have relied primarily on ambush and scavenging rather than active pursuit. This is a major shift in our understanding of these prehistoric creatures and how they interacted with their environments.

What the New Research Revealed About Animal Speed

The study found that Tyrannosaurus rex, long depicted as a terrifying pursuit predator capable of running at speeds of 40 to 55 kilometers per hour, likely moved at a maximum sustainable speed of 12 to 20 kilometers per hour. This is slightly faster than a fit human jogging pace, but far below the dramatic sprint speeds that made the species appear unstoppable in fictional portrayals. The revision stems from new understanding of the immense energetic cost of moving T-Rex's estimated 9,000-kilogram body mass at higher speeds.

Similarly, the study found that woolly mammoths had a maximum speed of approximately 16 to 20 kilometers per hour, consistent with the top speeds of modern elephants of comparable mass. This is a significant reduction from previously estimated speeds of 25 to 30 kilometers per hour.

The findings are based on a combination of skeletal measurements, muscle attachment site analysis, and comparison with modern animals of similar body mass. The researchers used these data to reconstruct the realistic locomotion capabilities of the species, providing a more accurate understanding of their behavior and ecology.

The Science Behind Speed Estimation from Fossils

The study's methodology is based on advanced computer modeling and biomechanical analysis, which allows researchers to estimate the energetic cost of movement in extinct species. This approach takes into account the size and shape of the animal's body, as well as the properties of its muscles and skeleton. By comparing these data with those of modern animals, the researchers can estimate the maximum sustainable speed of the extinct species.

The bone stress calculations indicate that femur fracture would have been likely at the running speeds previously assumed for Tyrannosaurus rex. This suggests that the species was not capable of sustaining high speeds over long distances, and instead relied on other strategies to hunt and interact with its environment.
