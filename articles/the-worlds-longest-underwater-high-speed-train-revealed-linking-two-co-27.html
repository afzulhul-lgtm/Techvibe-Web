The World’s Longest Underwater High-Speed Train Revealed: Linking Two Continents Beneath the Sea

By {{AUTHOR_NAME}} Senior Editor
Published: May 08, 2026 Updated: May 08, 2026 6 Min Read

A 103-kilometer underwater rail tunnel is being planned to connect the Spanish coast near Tarifa with the Moroccan coast near Tangier, beneath the Strait of Gibraltar. This ambitious project will enable trains to travel at speeds of up to 300 kilometers per hour, covering the crossing in approximately 20 minutes. Currently, the journey requires a 90-minute ferry crossing, which is subject to weather and scheduling constraints.

The project's significance lies in its potential to transform the way people and goods move between Europe and Africa. By providing a fast and reliable transportation link, it could boost trade, tourism, and economic growth in the region. Both Spain and Morocco have renewed their commitment to the project, which has been in discussion since the 1980s. The European Union has also identified the Gibraltar link as a priority infrastructure corridor.

What the Underwater Train Project Involves

The tunnel design features a twin-bore configuration with a separate service tunnel running between the two main tunnels. This design is similar to the Channel Tunnel connecting England and France. However, the Gibraltar Strait tunnel poses significant engineering challenges due to its maximum water depth of 300 meters, which is roughly three times deeper than the Channel Tunnel's maximum depth. The seabed geology also includes a complex zone of tectonic activity at the junction of the African and Eurasian plates.

Construction of the tunnel would require specialized tunnel boring machines capable of operating at unprecedented depths and pressures. The project's complexity and scale are unparalleled, making it a technically ambitious undertaking. The tunnel's design and construction will need to take into account the unique geological and environmental conditions of the Strait of Gibraltar.

The Engineering Challenges of Building Beneath the Sea

The engineering challenges of building the underwater tunnel are substantial. The maximum water depth of 300 meters and the complex seabed geology require specialized equipment and techniques. The tunnel boring machines will need to be designed to withstand the extreme pressures and conditions found at such depths. Additionally, the project will require careful planning and execution to minimize the environmental impact on the marine ecosystem.

The construction process is expected to be complex and time-consuming, with a projected completion date of no earlier than 2040. The total project cost is estimated to range from €15 billion to €25 billion, depending on the final alignment and design specifications. The cost and complexity of the project highlight the significant challenges and risks involved in building the world's longest underwater high-speed rail tunnel.
