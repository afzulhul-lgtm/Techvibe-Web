Trump Warns 'The Shootin' Starts' – Bigger, Better & Stronger Attack on Iran if They Don't Reopen Strait of Hormuz

By Sarah Mitchell Senior Editor
Published: April 18, 2026 Updated: April 18, 2026 12 Min Read

President Donald Trump issued a direct public ultimatum to Iran in April 2026: reopen the Strait of Hormuz to oil tanker traffic or face a military response he described as "bigger, better, and stronger" than previous US strikes. The warning came as the strait has been effectively closed since the outbreak of the US-Iran conflict in late February — a closure that has pushed Brent crude oil to approximately $100 per barrel, roughly 40 percent above pre-conflict prices.

The strait, a narrow chokepoint between the Omani and Iranian coastlines at its narrowest point of 21 miles, normally carries approximately 17 million barrels of oil and petroleum products per day. That represents about 21 percent of global petroleum liquids consumption. Its closure has been the single largest supply disruption in the history of global oil markets and the primary driver of energy price spikes being felt by consumers worldwide.

What Trump Said — The Exact Warning and Its Context

Trump's statement came during a White House press briefing in mid-April 2026, where he said that if Iran does not reopen the Strait of Hormuz immediately, "the shootin' starts — and it'll be bigger, better, and stronger than anything they've seen before." The remarks followed a period of limited US naval operations in the Gulf aimed at protecting commercial shipping that had largely failed to reopen the transit corridor.

The warning is consistent with Trump's established negotiating pattern of public escalation followed by back-channel diplomacy. US officials confirmed that indirect negotiations through Oman — the same channel used for prior nuclear talks — remain active. The public ultimatum is widely interpreted as pressure on Iran to negotiate from a position of urgency, though Iranian officials publicly rejected the framing, calling the threat "psychological warfare."

Trump also paused a naval mission to forcibly reopen the strait days after announcing it — a move that drew criticism from Republican hawks who argued it signaled weakness, and relief from European allies who feared uncontrolled escalation. The pause and the subsequent threat represent the same oscillating pressure strategy Trump applied in other geopolitical negotiations.

Why the Strait of Hormuz Matters: The Numbers Behind the Crisis

The Strait of Hormuz is the most strategically critical energy chokepoint on Earth. The US Energy Information Administration's data shows that approximately 17 million barrels of crude oil, condensate, and petroleum products transited the strait daily in 2024 — the last full year before the conflict. That's roughly 21 percent of global petroleum liquids consumption and close to 30 percent of all seaborne crude oil trade.

The countries most dependent on Hormuz transit are Japan, South Korea, India, and China — all of which receive the vast majority of their oil imports through the strait. Europe is less exposed but still significantly affected through global price transmission. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, and Kuwait all export the bulk of their oil through the strait as well, making the closure as economically damaging to US allies in the Gulf as it is to Asian importers.

Saudi Aramco's CEO Amin Nasser confirmed in Q1 2026 earnings that the company's east-west pipeline — running from the Gulf coast to the Red Sea port of Yanbu — has been operating at maximum capacity of 7 million barrels per day to offset the closure. But that pipeline can only partially compensate for the 17 million barrels normally moving through Hormuz. The deficit has been the primary driver of prices reaching $100 per barrel.

Why Iran Closed the Strait and What They Want

Iran's closure of the strait followed initial US military strikes in late February 2026 that targeted Iranian naval assets and missile infrastructure. Tehran's stated justification is that the strait closure is a defensive measure — an economic lever to deter further US strikes and force Washington back to the negotiating table. Iranian officials have framed the closure as proportionate to the economic pressure the US has applied through sanctions.

Iran's demands for reopening center on three points: an end to US military operations against Iranian territory, the lifting of sanctions on Iranian oil exports and the Iranian central bank, and US acknowledgment of Iran's right to develop civilian nuclear infrastructure. The US has refused the second and third conditions as preconditions for any ceasefire, insisting that sanctions relief must come as part of a verified nuclear agreement, not as a precondition for stopping the strait closure.

Iranian hardliners within the government have argued that the strait closure is Iran's most powerful remaining leverage and should not be surrendered without concrete US concessions. This internal political dynamic makes it difficult for Iranian moderates to agree to reopen the strait in exchange for anything less than guaranteed, verified relief from the most damaging sanctions.

The Ceasefire Collapse: What Happened and Who Is Blaming Whom

A temporary ceasefire negotiated through Oman in late March 2026 collapsed within days of taking effect. The US accused Iran of firing on a US surveillance drone operating over international waters hours after the ceasefire was announced. Iran denied the accusation and in turn accused the US of continuing covert operations targeting Iranian missile infrastructure despite the ceasefire terms.

Independent verification of either claim is impossible — both events allegedly occurred in areas without neutral observers. European diplomats involved in the Omani channel have described the collapse as consistent with the broader pattern of the conflict: both sides agree to pause while continuing activities each side considers permitted under its own interpretation of the ceasefire terms, leading to inevitable breakdown.

The US deployed an additional 3,000 troops to the region following the ceasefire collapse, primarily to reinforce naval assets in the Persian Gulf and to protect US installations in Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, and Qatar. This brings total additional US deployments in the region since the conflict began to approximately 15,000 personnel.

Key Takeaways

  • Trump threatened a "bigger, better, and stronger" military attack on Iran if the Strait of Hormuz is not reopened — while simultaneously keeping diplomatic channels through Oman open, reflecting his established pattern of public escalation alongside back-channel negotiation.
  • The Strait of Hormuz normally carries approximately 17 million barrels per day — about 21 percent of global petroleum liquids. Its closure has pushed Brent crude to $100 per barrel, roughly 40 percent above pre-conflict levels.
  • Iran's conditions for reopening the strait include an end to US military strikes, sanctions relief, and acknowledgment of nuclear rights — all of which the US has refused as preconditions, creating a structural deadlock that neither side has found a way to exit without appearing to capitulate.

How NATO and US Allies Are Responding

NATO's formal response has been constrained by alliance divisions. The United States, United Kingdom, and several Eastern European members have backed the US position that Iran's strait closure constitutes an illegal act under international maritime law and that forcible reopening is legally justified. France, Germany, Italy, and Spain have urged de-escalation, arguing that military action to reopen the strait could trigger a wider regional conflict with consequences far beyond the oil markets.

The European Union has separately opened its own diplomatic channel to Tehran, framing it as a civilian and economic dialogue distinct from the US-led military track. EU foreign policy chief Josep Borrell visited Muscat in late March to coordinate with Omani mediators, though the collapse of the ceasefire shortly afterward set those efforts back significantly.

Japan and South Korea — both heavily dependent on Gulf oil — have publicly called for a diplomatic resolution while quietly increasing their strategic petroleum reserve drawdowns to buffer against continued supply disruption. The International Energy Agency authorized member countries to release strategic reserves in March 2026, but the releases have had a limited dampening effect on prices given the scale of the supply disruption.

The trajectory of oil prices since the conflict began tells the story clearly:

At $100 per barrel, Brent crude is at its highest level since the 2022 energy crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Gasoline prices in the United States have risen approximately 35 to 45 cents per gallon depending on region. Jet fuel costs have risen sharply enough that several airlines have announced fare surcharges ranging from $15 to $50 per flight. Natural gas prices in Europe — which are linked to oil through energy market dynamics — have also risen significantly, increasing household energy bills heading into summer.

Saudi Aramco's CEO stated in a Bloomberg statement that even if the strait reopens immediately, it would take months for the oil market to rebalance, and if the disruption continues beyond a few more weeks, market normalization would not occur until 2027.

What Happens Next: Possible Scenarios

Three scenarios are in play, each with a meaningfully different probability based on current dynamics.

Scenario 1 — Negotiated limited deal (most likely near-term): Iran agrees to reopen the strait in exchange for a suspension of new sanctions and a US commitment to halt offensive strikes for a defined period. This would not resolve the nuclear issue or the underlying conflict but would provide economic relief to both sides. The Omani channel makes this technically feasible if political will exists on both sides.

Scenario 2 — Continued stalemate (moderately likely): Both sides maintain current positions for weeks or months while Iran's economy deteriorates further under sanctions and oil prices remain elevated. This is the current trajectory. The risk is that prolonged stalemate increases the probability of miscalculation or unintended escalation by either side.

Scenario 3 — Military escalation (lower probability but high impact): The US conducts a large-scale strike aimed at degrading Iran's ability to keep the strait closed — targeting naval assets, mine-laying capabilities, and shore-based anti-ship missile systems. Iran responds with attacks on US bases in the Gulf and potentially on Saudi and Emirati oil infrastructure. This scenario would cause oil prices to spike well above $130 per barrel and could tip the global economy into recession.

Frequently Asked Questions

What did Trump mean by 'The Shootin Starts' regarding Iran?

Trump warned that continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz would trigger a larger US military operation against Iran than prior strikes — which he described as "bigger, better, and stronger." The statement was accompanied by continued diplomatic activity through Oman, suggesting it is intended as leverage rather than an imminent operational declaration.

Why did Iran close the Strait of Hormuz?

Iran closed the strait following US military strikes on its naval and missile infrastructure in late February 2026. Tehran framed the closure as both a defensive measure and economic leverage to force the US back to negotiations. Iran is demanding an end to US strikes, sanctions relief, and acknowledgment of its nuclear rights — all of which the US has declined as preconditions.

How much oil passes through the Strait of Hormuz daily?

Approximately 17 million barrels of oil and petroleum products per day normally transit the strait, according to the US Energy Information Administration. This represents about 21 percent of global petroleum liquids consumption and roughly 30 percent of all seaborne oil trade. No alternative route can fully replace this volume.

What has happened to oil prices since the Strait of Hormuz closed?

Brent crude rose from approximately $66 per barrel in January 2026 to around $100 per barrel by April 2026 — a roughly 52 percent increase. Saudi Aramco's CEO confirmed the $100 per barrel level and warned that the market would take months to rebalance even after the strait reopens. Global gasoline prices have risen significantly, with US pump prices up 35 to 45 cents per gallon.

Will the US launch a bigger military attack on Iran?

Trump's threat is real but the decision would depend heavily on Iran's response and the diplomatic situation. The Omani channel remains active, giving both sides an off-ramp. European allies have urged against unilateral military escalation. The most likely near-term scenario is continued pressure combined with renewed negotiation rather than immediate military escalation, though the risk increases with each week the stalemate continues.

Conclusion

Trump's "bigger, better, stronger" warning is the sharpest escalation in US rhetoric since the conflict began — but it arrives alongside continued diplomatic activity that suggests neither side has fully closed the door on a negotiated exit from the stalemate. The strait closure has already achieved Iran's goal of imposing severe economic pain: $100 per barrel oil, disrupted global supply chains, and intense diplomatic pressure on the United States from allies who depend on Gulf energy.

The problem for both sides is that the cost of continuing to hold current positions keeps rising. Iran's economy is under severe sanction pressure. The US faces domestic political costs from elevated gasoline prices. The world economy is absorbing the shock. Whether Trump's ultimatum accelerates a breakthrough or a breakdown will depend on whether either side finds an exit ramp it can take without appearing to have blinked first — and in this conflict, that calculation is the hardest one of all.
