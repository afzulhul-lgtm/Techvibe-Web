Dual-Use Tech: How Companies Sell the Same Tools for Aid and War

By Sarah Mitchell Senior Editor
Published: May 09, 2026 Updated: May 09, 2026 7 Min Read

After an earthquake or a wildfire, the modern disaster response arrives with satellite images, heat maps, and data dashboards that promise clarity in chaos. Those tools can be genuinely useful. But using them effectively requires trust from affected populations — trust that the technology is there to help them, not to surveil or target them. That trust is getting harder to maintain.

The problem isn't that dual-use technology exists. It always has. The problem is that the same companies are increasingly selling identical core platforms to humanitarian organizations and to military and intelligence agencies — then pointing to the humanitarian deployment when anyone asks what kind of world they're building.

What Is Dual-Use Technology and Why It Matters Now

Dual-use technology — goods, software, and systems that serve both civilian and military purposes — has always existed. Satellites, GPS, and the internet all began as military projects. But dual-use has become something different now: a deliberate commercial strategy. Build a general-purpose platform for processing information and generating action-oriented outputs. Sell it everywhere. When critics ask uncomfortable questions, point to the humanitarian deployment.

The harder problem, as researchers and humanitarian practitioners have noted, is that the same data-integration logic that optimizes food distribution in a crisis becomes a targeting tool in a conflict. It's the same code. The same vendor. The same support contract. And when a humanitarian agency depends on that vendor, it cannot simply ignore what else the platform does.

A British surgeon who testified before the UK Parliament in 2026 described patients in Gaza — including children — who recounted drones arriving after an initial airstrike to locate and shoot injured survivors individually on the ground. If aerial surveillance technology is deployed both to assess humanitarian damage and to conduct follow-up strikes in the same conflict zone, injured civilians have no way to distinguish the two. That is the real-world consequence of the humanitarian halo problem.

Palantir and WFP: When Aid Operations Meet Intelligence Systems

The World Food Program announced its partnership with Palantir in 2019 as part of a digital transformation push, with the stated goal of improving real-time logistics and decision-making for food assistance. The argument was straightforward: better data integration moves food and cash to hungry people faster.

The backlash was equally swift. Human rights groups, humanitarian organizations, and technology ethics researchers raised immediate objections about linking a UN relief agency to a company with deep ties to US intelligence agencies and immigration enforcement. The concern wasn't that WFP staff were building targeting pipelines. It was simpler: once you depend on a vendor whose primary revenue comes from security agencies, you cannot credibly separate yourself from what that vendor does.

Palantir's defense portfolio is not peripheral to its business. In March 2026, Reuters reported that the US Department of Defense designated Palantir's Maven system as a program of record — locking it in as a funded, ongoing core military capability. The same report stated that Maven analyzes battlefield data to identify potential targets and had supported thousands of targeted strikes in recent weeks. That is the same data-integration and decision-support architecture that WFP deployed for supply chain optimization. One context feeds people. The other directs strikes. The code travels easily between them.

Satellite Imagery Firms Maxar and Planet: One Supply Chain, Two Very Different Masters

UNOSAT, the UN's satellite analysis center, provides imagery analysis for humanitarian emergencies — damage assessments, displacement mapping, flood extent analysis. The work is credible and valuable. But the commercial satellite imagery market that UNOSAT and other humanitarian organizations draw from is deeply entangled with national security procurement.

In 2024, the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency awarded Maxar a roughly $359 million Commercial GEOINT Access Portal contract — a straightforward acknowledgment that the same imagery supply chain powering humanitarian flood maps also feeds US intelligence workflows. Then in March 2025, Reuters reported that the US government temporarily disabled Ukraine's access to Maxar satellite imagery through a US government platform, with the NGA confirming the action.

That incident is worth sitting with. A geopolitical decision — made somewhere between a government agency and a commercial vendor — turned off a country's satellite visibility. If humanitarian verification systems are built on top of that same chokepoint, humanitarian sight can be throttled by the same lever. Planet presents a nearly identical pattern. Its Rapid Response program delivers satellite data to humanitarian organizations during disasters. In 2025, Planet also announced selection for a NATO contract to deliver advanced daily monitoring and intelligence capabilities. One sensor network. Two entirely different purposes running in parallel.

Key Takeaways

  • Palantir's Maven system — the same platform architecture used in WFP's humanitarian partnership — was designated a US military program of record in March 2026 and has supported thousands of targeted strikes.
  • In March 2025, the US government temporarily cut Ukraine's access to Maxar satellite imagery, demonstrating that commercial humanitarian visibility tools can be throttled by geopolitical decisions.
  • Planet sells both humanitarian Rapid Response imagery and a NATO intelligence monitoring contract — one company, one sensor infrastructure, two entirely different operational purposes running simultaneously.

Starlink in Ukraine: From Disaster Connectivity to Strategic Leverage

SpaceX marketed Starlink for emergency response from early in its deployment, including sending terminals to vetted humanitarian organizations. That use is real. But the Belfer Center's case study on Starlink's role in Ukraine documented a more complicated picture: a dependency that exposed the fragility of outsourcing critical national connectivity to a private actor, and the political leverage that follows from that reliance.

Reports emerged suggesting that Starlink's availability had been manipulated — or at least questioned — in relation to a military offensive in progress. Whether true or not, the dynamic it revealed is the point: when a private satellite internet service can plausibly shape the outcome of a military operation, the infrastructure is no longer just support. It's governance. And humanitarian organizations relying on the same terminals for the same connectivity in the same conflict zone are caught in that same political calculus.

The Oslo Guidelines — longstanding guidance on the use of military and civil defense assets in humanitarian response — warn explicitly that UN agencies should avoid dependency on military resources. The dual-use vendor problem is the updated version of that warning. The dependency hasn't moved from helicopters and convoys into data platforms, satellite tasking, and proprietary analytics.

How Humanitarian Organizations Can Break the Dependency Cycle

The starting point is refusing to let humanitarian deployments serve as reputational cover for the rest of a vendor's portfolio. That requires structural changes in procurement, not just policy statements.

Humanitarian agencies and their donors should require dual-use disclosure as a standard procurement condition: if a vendor sells the same platform family for defense, intelligence, or domestic security, that must be disclosed upfront — not discovered later through investigative reporting. Contracts should include purpose limitations prohibiting downstream security use of beneficiary or operational data, clear data retention limits, and independent audit rights. Any vendor that refuses those terms is signaling that the partnership isn't genuine.

Exit strategies matter as much as entry conditions. Interoperable standards and data migration capacity ensure that "we can't leave this vendor" doesn't quietly become the permanent justification for continued dependency. None of this requires abandoning useful technology. It requires treating humanitarian deployment as the highest standard of restriction — not as a moral license to deploy anything and worry about the rest later.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does dual-use technology mean in humanitarian aid work?

Dual-use technology refers to tools, software, and systems that serve both civilian and military purposes. In humanitarian contexts this includes satellite imagery platforms, data analytics systems like Palantir, and connectivity infrastructure like Starlink — all of which aid organizations use for disaster response, and which the same vendors also sell to military and intelligence agencies for targeting, surveillance, and operations.

Is Palantir's Maven system used for military targeting and strikes?

Yes. Reuters reported in March 2026 that the US Department of Defense designated Palantir's Maven system as a program of record, locking it in as a funded ongoing military capability. The report stated Maven analyzes battlefield data to identify potential targets and has supported thousands of targeted strikes. Palantir simultaneously operates data partnerships with humanitarian organizations including the World Food Program.

How does Starlink's role in Ukraine affect humanitarian trust?

The Belfer Center's case study on Starlink in Ukraine documented how dependency on a private provider for critical connectivity exposed operations to political leverage. Reports suggested Starlink's availability may have been manipulated to influence a military offensive. Humanitarian organizations using the same infrastructure in the same conflict zone cannot credibly claim operational separation from those political dynamics.

What is the humanitarian halo effect and why is it dangerous?

The humanitarian halo effect is when a technology company uses its visible humanitarian deployments as reputational cover while simultaneously selling the same core platform for military targeting, intelligence gathering, or domestic enforcement. It's dangerous because it lets companies deflect scrutiny of their defense work by pointing to aid applications, while humanitarian organizations bear the trust and physical safety consequences of that association.

Can humanitarian agencies demand dual-use disclosure from tech vendors?

Yes, and procurement experts argue they must. Contracts should require vendors to disclose whether the same platform family is sold for defense, intelligence, or domestic security. They should also include purpose limitations banning downstream security use of beneficiary data, retention limits, and independent audit rights. Vendors who refuse these terms demonstrate the partnership's terms favor the vendor, not the humanitarian mission.

Conclusion

The question was never whether dual-use tools can do good in humanitarian settings. They can. Satellite imagery has saved lives in disaster response. Data integration has made food distribution faster. The question is whether we treat humanitarian deployment as a governing standard — a floor that restricts what the same technology can be used for elsewhere — or as a marketing credential that insulates vendors from scrutiny of everything else they sell.

Right now it functions mostly as the latter. The $359 million NGA contract, the Maven program of record, the NATO Planet deal, the Starlink leverage in Ukraine — these aren't failures of individual companies. They're the predictable result of a procurement system that never required vendors to choose. Until humanitarian agencies make dual-use disclosure and purpose limitations a baseline condition of procurement, the halo will keep doing the governance work it was never designed to do.
