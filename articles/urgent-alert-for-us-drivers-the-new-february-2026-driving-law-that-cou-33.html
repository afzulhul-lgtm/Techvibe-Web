Urgent Alert for U.S. Drivers: The New February 2026 Driving Law That Could Mean Jail Time

By {{AUTHOR_NAME}} Senior Editor
Published: May 08, 2026 Updated: May 08, 2026 5 Min Read

As of January 2026, several U.S. states have introduced new driving laws that significantly increase the penalties for certain traffic offenses, with some behaviors now punishable by jail time. For instance, in Georgia, Ohio, and Tennessee, using a handheld mobile device while driving is now a criminal misdemeanor, carrying fines of $500 to $1,000 for first-time offenders and mandatory minimum jail sentences of 10 to 30 days for repeat violations within 24 months.

The introduction of these laws is significant, as they reflect a growing concern about the risks associated with distracted driving and other hazardous behaviors on the road. With the new laws now in effect, drivers must be aware of the changes and take steps to ensure they are complying with the updated regulations to avoid hefty fines and potential jail time.

What the New February 2026 Driving Law Says

The new driving laws, which took effect in early 2026, focus on several key areas, including handheld mobile device use while driving, aggressive driving, and street racing. In addition to the reclassification of handheld mobile device use as a criminal misdemeanor, the laws also introduce harsher penalties for aggressive driving and street racing. For example, in Florida and several other states, extreme speeding – defined as traveling more than 50 mph above the posted speed limit or any speed above 100 mph – now triggers automatic vehicle impoundment and mandatory license suspension alongside criminal charges.

Furthermore, the laws have also updated the penalties for drunk driving, with multiple states now carrying lower blood alcohol content thresholds for enhanced penalties. These changes aim to reduce the number of accidents and fatalities on the road by deterring drivers from engaging in high-risk behaviors.

Which States Have Adopted the New Law

Several states have adopted the new driving laws, including Georgia, Ohio, Tennessee, and Florida. These states have introduced the new regulations as part of a broader effort to improve road safety and reduce the number of accidents and fatalities. Drivers should review their home state's 2026 traffic law updates through their state DMV website, as changes vary significantly by jurisdiction and enforcement began immediately upon the effective dates.
