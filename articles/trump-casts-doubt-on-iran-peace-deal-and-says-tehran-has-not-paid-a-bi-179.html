Trump casts doubt on Iran peace deal and says Tehran has not paid a big enough price

By Sarah Mitchell Senior Editor
Published: May 06, 2026 Updated: May 06, 2026 7 Min Read

President Donald Trump publicly cast doubt on the US-Iran nuclear agreement in early May 2026, stating that Tehran has not paid a big enough price for its past behavior and suggesting the current deal may not meet the bar he considers acceptable. The comments came as US and Iranian negotiators were in indirect talks mediated by Oman, with a preliminary framework reportedly close to completion.

The remarks immediately complicated the diplomatic picture. Iranian state media reported them within hours, and hardliners in Tehran used the statement to argue that any deal with the Trump administration is inherently unstable — a talking point that complicates the position of Iranian moderates who have backed engagement.

What Trump Said About the Peace Deal

Trump's remarks were made in the context of ongoing questions about whether a preliminary nuclear framework negotiated through Omani intermediaries goes far enough. He did not reject the talks outright but indicated that any final deal would need to include concessions beyond those currently on the table — specifically referencing Iran's ballistic missile program and its support for proxy forces in the region, neither of which Iran has agreed to include in nuclear negotiations.

The phrase "not paid a big enough price" reflects a consistent position Trump has held since withdrawing from the original Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action in May 2018. His administration's "maximum pressure" campaign — which reimposed sweeping sanctions on Iran's oil exports, banking sector, and central bank — was designed to force broader concessions than the 2015 deal required. The current talks are taking place against a backdrop of that pressure, with Iran's economy severely constrained.

Trump's comments did not specify what additional concessions would satisfy him, which is itself a negotiating dynamic. Ambiguity about the finish line keeps pressure on Tehran to offer more, though it also makes it harder for Iranian negotiators to sell any agreement domestically.

Why He Says Tehran Has Not Paid Enough

The "price" argument has two components. First, there's the nuclear file itself. Iran's uranium enrichment has reached 60 percent purity — below the 90 percent weapons-grade threshold but significantly above the 3.67 percent limit set by the 2015 JCPOA. The IAEA reported in early 2026 that Iran's stockpile of enriched uranium has grown to levels that would allow it to produce enough fissile material for multiple weapons within weeks if it chose to further enrich. From Trump's perspective, Iran's nuclear advances since 2018 represent leverage that must be fully surrendered before sanctions are lifted.

Second, there's the regional dimension. Iran's support for Hezbollah in Lebanon, the Houthis in Yemen, and various armed groups in Iraq and Syria has continued regardless of nuclear talks. Trump has consistently argued — as have Israeli officials — that a nuclear deal that ignores these activities effectively subsidizes them through sanctions relief without addressing the broader threat picture.

Iran has categorically refused to include either ballistic missiles or regional policy in nuclear negotiations, arguing these are separate matters of national defense and sovereignty. That gap between what Washington demands and what Tehran will discuss is the core structural problem in the talks.

What the Current Deal Status Actually Is

The current framework under discussion is a significantly more limited agreement than the 2015 JCPOA. Rather than a comprehensive long-term deal, US and Iranian officials have been discussing a shorter interim arrangement — sometimes described as a "freeze for freeze" — in which Iran would cap uranium enrichment at current levels and allow expanded IAEA access, in exchange for partial sanctions relief on specific sectors like oil and banking.

The IAEA's ability to monitor Iran's nuclear sites has been significantly degraded since Iran restricted inspector access in 2021, removing surveillance cameras from several facilities. Restoring that monitoring infrastructure is a US precondition for any sanctions relief, and the extent to which Iran will allow IAEA access back into its program is a key unresolved issue in the current talks.

European parties — the UK, France, and Germany — have been pushing to preserve some version of a nuclear agreement, fearing that a complete collapse of talks would accelerate Iran's enrichment toward weapons-grade levels and potentially trigger Israeli military action against Iranian nuclear facilities.

Key Takeaways

  • Trump said Tehran has not paid a big enough price, casting doubt on a preliminary framework being negotiated through Omani intermediaries in May 2026.
  • The core sticking point is the same as in 2018: the US wants Iran's ballistic missile program and regional proxy activities included in any deal, and Iran refuses to negotiate those issues alongside nuclear ones.
  • Iran's uranium enrichment has reached 60 percent purity, and its stockpile has grown significantly since 2018 — giving Tehran real nuclear leverage while simultaneously making the threat calculus more urgent for Washington and its allies.

How Iran Has Officially Responded

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi responded cautiously, affirming that Tehran remains committed to diplomacy but warning that the US cannot keep shifting the goalposts. He specifically rejected any suggestion that Iran would include its missile program or regional policy in nuclear negotiations, calling those "red lines" that reflect Iran's sovereign defense decisions.

The Iranian response also carried an implicit warning: if talks collapse, Iran will resume its nuclear program without restrictions, and the international community will bear responsibility for that outcome. This framing is designed to shift pressure back onto Washington and onto European capitals that have urged Iran to remain at the table.

Behind the official statements, Iranian negotiators have signaled through intermediaries that they believe a limited interim agreement is still achievable, but that it requires the US to accept a narrower scope — nuclear only — and to provide meaningful sanctions relief upfront rather than staged relief tied to verification milestones. That sequencing dispute is a recurring obstacle in US-Iran negotiations.

What Comes Next for US-Iran Relations

The most likely near-term scenario is continued indirect negotiations through Oman, with both sides managing domestic political pressures while keeping the channel open. A full diplomatic collapse is in neither side's immediate interest — Iran faces economic pressure from sanctions, and the US faces the risk that a breakdown accelerates Iran's nuclear program toward a threshold that forces a military response.

The more dangerous scenario is a gradual erosion of the talks without a formal breakdown — a prolonged stalemate in which Iran continues incremental nuclear advances and the US continues applying pressure, until one side or the other concludes that the cost of the current trajectory exceeds the cost of escalation. That assessment could change quickly if Iran enriches to weapons-grade levels or if Israel decides to act unilaterally against Iranian nuclear facilities, either of which would create immediate pressure on Washington to respond.

For now, Oman's role as the intermediary channel remains intact, and both sides have incentives to keep it that way. But Trump's public comments about Iran not paying enough make it harder for Iranian negotiators to justify continued engagement to their domestic audience — which is precisely why such remarks, whatever their intended audience, carry real diplomatic consequences.

Conclusion

Trump's remarks about Iran not paying a big enough price are not a departure from his long-held position — they're a consistent extension of it. The gap between what Washington demands and what Tehran will discuss has existed since 2018. What changes is the context: Iran's nuclear program is significantly more advanced now than when Trump first withdrew from the JCPOA, which means the stakes of a failed negotiation are higher for everyone involved.

The Omani channel remains the best available path to any agreement, however limited. Whether Trump's public skepticism reflects a genuine negotiating posture or a signal that the administration is walking away is the central question that European and regional partners are now trying to answer through their own back channels with both Washington and Tehran.
