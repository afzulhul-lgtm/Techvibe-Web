Try These 5 Bedtime Yoga Poses for a Better Nights Sleep

By {{AUTHOR_NAME}} Senior Editor
Published: May 08, 2026 Updated: May 08, 2026 7 Min Read

Hundreds of millions of people worldwide suffer from sleep disorders and poor sleep quality, with many seeking solutions to improve their sleep. Research published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine has found that practicing yoga within 30 to 60 minutes of bedtime can significantly reduce the time required to fall asleep and increase total sleep duration. This is because certain yoga poses target the physiological mechanisms underlying poor sleep, helping to calm the mind and body before sleep.

The importance of addressing sleep disorders cannot be overstated, as poor sleep quality can have significant impacts on physical and mental health. By incorporating a short bedtime yoga practice into their daily routine, individuals can take a proactive approach to improving their sleep quality and overall well-being. With just 15 to 20 minutes of practice per night, individuals can experience meaningful improvements in sleep onset time and sleep depth.

Why Yoga Before Bed Improves Sleep Quality

Yoga practiced before bed can have a significant impact on sleep quality due to its ability to activate the parasympathetic nervous system, also known as the body's rest-and-digest mode. This system helps to calm the mind and body, reducing stress and anxiety that can interfere with sleep. By practicing yoga within 30 to 60 minutes of bedtime, individuals can signal to their body that it is time to sleep, helping to regulate their sleep-wake cycle.

The five poses presented here are specifically designed to target the physiological mechanisms underlying poor sleep. Each pose should be held for two to five minutes, with slow and natural breathing. This helps to quiet the mind, reduce physical tension, and prepare the body for sleep. By incorporating these poses into a bedtime yoga practice, individuals can take a proactive approach to improving their sleep quality and overall well-being.

Pose 1 — Legs Up the Wall for Nervous System Reset

The first pose, known as Legs Up the Wall or Viparita Karani, is a gentle inversion that reverses blood flow from the legs, reduces heart rate, and activates the parasympathetic nervous system. This pose can be practiced by sitting with your back against a wall, then lifting your legs up against the wall, with your body forming an L-shape. By holding this pose for two to five minutes, individuals can help to calm their nervous system and prepare their body for sleep.

This pose is particularly useful for individuals who experience stress and anxiety before bed, as it helps to calm the mind and body. By practicing Legs Up the Wall, individuals can signal to their body that it is time to relax and prepare for sleep, helping to improve sleep quality and duration.

Pose 2 — Supine Twist to Release Spinal Tension

The second pose, known as the Supine Twist, is a gentle twist that helps to release tension accumulated in the back muscles during the day. This pose can be practiced by lying on your back, with your knees bent and feet flat on the floor, then twisting your torso to one side, with your arms extended. By holding this pose for two to five minutes, individuals can help to release physical tension and prepare their body for sleep.

This pose is particularly useful for individuals who experience back pain or discomfort before bed, as it helps to release tension and reduce inflammation. By practicing the Supine Twist, individuals can help to improve their sleep quality and reduce the risk of sleep disorders.

Pose 3 — Happy Baby Pose for Hip and Lower Back Release

The third pose, known as Happy Baby Pose, is a gentle stretch that helps to open the hips and sacroiliac joints, areas where many people unconsciously hold stress. This pose can be practiced by lying on your back, with your knees bent and feet flexed, then lifting your legs up towards your chest, with your hands holding onto your knees. By holding this pose for two to five minutes, individuals can help to release physical tension and prepare their body for sleep.

This pose is particularly useful for individuals who experience hip or lower back pain before bed, as it helps to release tension and reduce inflammation. By practicing Happy Baby Pose, individuals can help to improve their sleep quality and reduce the risk of sleep disorders.

Pose 4 — Seated Forward Fold to Calm the Mind

The fourth pose, known as Seated Forward Fold, is a gentle stretch that helps to calm the mind and quiet the nervous system. This pose can be practiced by sitting on the floor, with your legs extended in front of you, then folding your torso forward, with your arms extended. By holding this pose for two to five minutes, individuals can help to calm their mind and prepare their body for sleep.

This pose is particularly useful for individuals who experience stress and anxiety before bed, as it helps to calm the mind and reduce mental activity. By practicing Seated Forward Fold, individuals can help to improve their sleep quality and reduce the risk of sleep disorders.

Pose 5 — Corpse Pose for Full Body Relaxation

The final pose, known as Corpse Pose or Savasana, is a gentle relaxation pose that helps to train the body and mind to fully surrender physical tension. This pose can be practiced by lying on your back, with your arms and legs relaxed, and your eyes closed. By holding this pose for two to five minutes, individuals can help to calm their mind and prepare their body for sleep.

This pose is particularly useful for individuals who experience difficulty relaxing before bed, as it helps to release physical tension and calm the mind. By practicing Corpse Pose, individuals can help to improve their sleep quality and reduce the risk of sleep disorders.

Key Takeaways

  • Practicing yoga within 30 to 60 minutes of bedtime can significantly improve sleep quality and duration.
  • The five poses presented here can help to calm the mind, release physical tension, and prepare the body for sleep.
  • Each pose should be held for two to five minutes, with slow and natural breathing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should I practice the bedtime yoga poses?

You should practice the bedtime yoga poses for 15 to 20 minutes per night, holding each pose for two to five minutes. This can help to improve sleep quality and duration.

What is the best time to practice the bedtime yoga poses?

The best time to practice the bedtime yoga poses is within 30 to 60 minutes of bedtime, as this can help to signal to your body that it is time to sleep.

Can I practice the bedtime yoga poses if I have a medical condition?

Yes, you can practice the bedtime yoga poses if you have a medical condition, but it is recommended that you consult with a healthcare professional before starting a new exercise routine. This can help to ensure that you are practicing the poses safely and effectively.

How long does it take to see improvements in sleep quality?

It can take one to two weeks of consistent practice to see meaningful improvements in sleep quality and duration. It is recommended that you practice the bedtime yoga poses nightly to experience the full benefits.

Conclusion

By incorporating a short bedtime yoga practice into your daily routine, you can take a proactive approach to improving your sleep quality and overall well-being. The five poses presented here can help to calm the mind, release physical tension, and prepare the body for sleep. With just 15 to 20 minutes of practice per night, you can experience meaningful improvements in sleep onset time and sleep depth.

Remember to practice the poses consistently, holding each pose for two to five minutes, and breathing slowly and naturally. By doing so, you can help to improve your sleep quality, reduce the risk of sleep disorders, and wake up feeling refreshed and revitalized. With patience and consistency, you can experience the full benefits of a bedtime yoga practice and improve your overall health and well-being.
