Over 65 and Losing Flexibility? New Study Reveals It's NOT Your Muscles — Here Is The Shocking Truth

By Dr. Amara Singh Senior Editor
Published: May 06, 2026 Updated: May 06, 2026 6 Min Read

As we age, our bodies undergo a series of changes that can affect our physical abilities, including flexibility. For individuals over 65, losing flexibility can significantly impact their quality of life, making everyday activities more challenging. However, a new study suggests that the primary cause of this loss in flexibility may not be due to muscle issues, as previously thought. Instead, research points to the role of connective tissue, specifically the extracellular matrix, in this process.

The extracellular matrix, composed of various proteins and polysaccharides, provides structural support to cells and tissues. In the context of flexibility, the extracellular matrix plays a crucial role in maintaining the integrity and elasticity of connective tissue. As we age, the extracellular matrix undergoes changes, including the accumulation of advanced glycosylation end-products (AGEs), which can lead to stiffness and reduced flexibility. This process is similar to what is observed in the formation of atherosclerotic plaques, where the accumulation of AGEs contributes to vascular stiffness.

Understanding the role of the extracellular matrix in flexibility loss is essential for developing effective strategies to maintain or improve flexibility in older adults. Research has shown that certain enzymes, such as matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), play a crucial role in remodeling the extracellular matrix. MMPs are involved in the breakdown and degradation of extracellular matrix components, which can help maintain tissue elasticity. However, an imbalance in MMP activity can lead to excessive degradation, contributing to tissue stiffness and reduced flexibility.

Studies have also investigated the effects of exercise and physical activity on the extracellular matrix and flexibility. For example, a study published in the Journal of Applied Physiology found that resistance training can increase the expression of MMPs, leading to improved flexibility and reduced stiffness in older adults. Additionally, research on the effects of yoga and Pilates on flexibility has shown that these exercises can improve flexibility by enhancing the elasticity of connective tissue.

Furthermore, the discovery of the role of certain nutrients, such as vitamin C and hyaluronic acid, in maintaining the health of the extracellular matrix has provided new avenues for promoting flexibility. Vitamin C, for example, is essential for the synthesis of collagen, a key component of the extracellular matrix, while hyaluronic acid can help maintain the hydration and elasticity of connective tissue. A diet rich in these nutrients, combined with regular exercise, can help support the health of the extracellular matrix and promote flexibility.

Step-by-Step Guide to Get Started

To improve flexibility, it is essential to adopt a comprehensive approach that incorporates exercise, nutrition, and lifestyle modifications. A step-by-step guide to getting started includes:

  • Consulting with a healthcare professional to develop a personalized exercise plan, focusing on activities that promote flexibility, such as yoga or Pilates.
  • Incorporating exercises that target the extracellular matrix, such as resistance training, to improve tissue elasticity.
  • Maintaining a balanced diet rich in nutrients essential for extracellular matrix health, including vitamin C and hyaluronic acid.

Additionally, staying hydrated and avoiding activities that can lead to excessive wear and tear on connective tissue, such as high-impact exercise, can help promote flexibility. By adopting these strategies, individuals can take proactive steps to maintain or improve their flexibility, even as they age.

How to Practice Safely Without Injury

When engaging in exercises aimed at improving flexibility, it is crucial to practice safely to avoid injury. This includes:

  • Warming up before exercise to prepare the muscles and connective tissue.
  • Using proper technique and form to avoid putting excessive stress on joints and tissues.
  • Listening to the body and stopping if pain or discomfort occurs.

Furthermore, incorporating exercises that strengthen the muscles surrounding joints, such as the rotator cuff muscles in the shoulder, can help improve joint stability and reduce the risk of injury. By practicing safely and being mindful of the body's limitations, individuals can enjoy the benefits of exercise while minimizing the risk of injury.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the extracellular matrix, and how does it affect flexibility?

The extracellular matrix is a network of proteins and polysaccharides that provides structural support to cells and tissues. Changes in the extracellular matrix, such as the accumulation of advanced glycosylation end-products (AGEs), can lead to stiffness and reduced flexibility.

Can exercise improve flexibility in older adults?

Yes, exercise can improve flexibility in older adults by maintaining the health of the extracellular matrix and promoting tissue elasticity. Resistance training, yoga, and Pilates are examples of exercises that can help improve flexibility.

What nutrients are essential for maintaining the health of the extracellular matrix?

Nutrients such as vitamin C and hyaluronic acid are essential for maintaining the health of the extracellular matrix. Vitamin C is necessary for collagen synthesis, while hyaluronic acid helps maintain the hydration and elasticity of connective tissue.

Conclusion

As we age, maintaining flexibility is essential for preserving our quality of life. By understanding the role of the extracellular matrix in flexibility loss, we can develop targeted strategies to promote flexibility and reduce the risk of injury. A comprehensive approach that incorporates exercise, nutrition, and lifestyle modifications can help support the health of the extracellular matrix and maintain flexibility, even in older adults.

Further research is needed to fully understand the mechanisms underlying flexibility loss and to develop effective interventions. However, by adopting a proactive approach to maintaining flexibility, individuals can take control of their health and enjoy the benefits of exercise and physical activity, regardless of age. The discovery of the role of the extracellular matrix in flexibility loss has opened new avenues for promoting flexibility and reducing the risk of injury, and it is essential to continue exploring this area of research to improve our understanding of this complex process.
