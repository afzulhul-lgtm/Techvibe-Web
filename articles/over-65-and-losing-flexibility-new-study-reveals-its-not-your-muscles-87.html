Over 65 and Losing Flexibility? New Study Reveals It's NOT Your Muscles — Here Is The Shocking Truth

By Dr. Amara Singh Senior Editor
Published: May 10, 2026 Updated: May 10, 2026 4 Min Read

Over losing flexibility new research is shedding light on a surprising culprit: connective tissue. The primary cause of age-related flexibility loss in adults over 65 is not muscle stiffness, as commonly believed, but rather changes in the connective tissue — specifically fascia and tendons — which become less hydrated and elastic over time.

But why does this matter? It's hard to say exactly why, but understanding the root cause of flexibility loss is crucial for developing effective interventions. That's where a study published in the Journal of Physiology in 2025 comes in, providing valuable insights into the relationship between connective tissue and flexibility.

What Makes This Approach Effective

The research team at Stanford University School of Medicine examined biopsies from 210 participants aged 55 to 75, comparing connective tissue samples from highly active and sedentary individuals. They found that the collagen fibers in fascia became increasingly cross-linked with age, reducing their ability to slide past one another during movement. This cross-linking was significantly less pronounced in participants who practiced consistent stretching, yoga, or mobility exercises at least three times per week.

So, what's the takeaway? From what's been reported so far, it seems that targeted mobility work can improve the hydration and organization of fascial tissue, even in older adults. Dynamic stretching before activity and prolonged static stretching of 60 seconds or more after exercise were both shown to improve fascial elasticity over a 12-week intervention.

Here's the thing: yoga and Pilates showed the strongest results in the study. Hydration also plays a significant role, as fascia is approximately 70 percent water. The researchers recommended that older adults prioritize morning mobility routines before the fascial tissue tightens through the day's inactivity.

Step-by-Step Guide to Get Started

Getting started with a mobility routine can seem daunting, but it doesn't have to be. Begin by incorporating dynamic stretching into your daily routine, focusing on movements that promote flexibility and range of motion. You can start with simple exercises like leg swings, arm circles, and torso twists.

And, as you progress, you can add more complex movements like yoga or Pilates. The key is to find activities that you enjoy and that challenge your connective tissue in a positive way. That's where the peer-reviewed study comes in, providing a solid foundation for developing effective interventions.

How to Practice Safely Without Injury

Practicing mobility exercises safely is crucial to avoiding injury and promoting long-term flexibility. To put that in perspective, it's essential to listen to your body and not push yourself too hard, especially if you're just starting out. Start with gentle exercises and gradually increase the intensity and duration as you become more comfortable.

What that means, practically speaking, is that you should focus on controlled movements and avoid bouncing or jerking motions. It's also essential to warm up before exercising and cool down afterwards to prevent muscle soreness and promote recovery.

Key Takeaways

  • Connective tissue, not muscle stiffness, is the primary cause of age-related flexibility loss.
  • Targeted mobility work can improve the hydration and organization of fascial tissue.
  • Dynamic stretching, yoga, and Pilates can help improve fascial elasticity.
