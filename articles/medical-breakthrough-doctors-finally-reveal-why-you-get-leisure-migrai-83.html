What the Study or Report Found

During periods of stress, the body releases elevated cortisol, which suppresses the inflammatory processes that trigger migraines. When stress suddenly drops, cortisol levels fall rapidly, triggering a rebound inflammatory response and a drop in serotonin levels that sets off the migraine cascade. This is a critical finding, as it explains the underlying mechanism of leisure migraines, and it's a key aspect of the medical breakthrough doctors finally discovered.

A study involving over 400 migraine patients found that approximately 17 percent reported significantly more frequent migraine episodes on weekends or at the start of holidays. The study's findings are crucial in understanding the impact of stress and relaxation on migraine episodes, and they provide valuable insights for the development of treatment guidelines.

Who Is Affected and How

Neurologists point to behavioral changes on days off, such as irregular sleep schedules, sleeping in or disrupted sleep, caffeine withdrawal when people skip their workday morning coffee, different eating patterns, and reduced physical activity, as compounding triggers. Dr. Noah Rosen, director of the Headache Center at Northwell Health, describes these as 'trigger stacking' events where multiple small changes combine to cross the threshold for a migraine attack. This is a critical aspect of the medical breakthrough doctors finally achieved, as it highlights the importance of lifestyle factors in triggering migraines.
