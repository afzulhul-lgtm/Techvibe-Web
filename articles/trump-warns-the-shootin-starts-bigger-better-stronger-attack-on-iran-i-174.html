Trump Warns 'The Shootin' Starts' – Bigger, Better & Stronger Attack on Iran if They Don't Reopen Strait of Hormuz

By Sarah Mitchell Senior Editor
Published: May 06, 2026 Updated: May 06, 2026 9 Min Read

President Trump issued a public ultimatum to Iran in May 2026: reopen the Strait of Hormuz or face a military response he described as "bigger, better, and stronger" than anything the US has done so far. The warning came as the strait — the world's most critical oil shipping chokepoint — has been effectively closed since the US-Iran conflict began in late February, sending global oil prices to approximately $100 per barrel and triggering the worst energy supply disruption in modern history.

The conflict, which started after US strikes on Iranian naval and missile infrastructure, has created a standoff that neither side has found an exit from. A temporary ceasefire brokered through Oman collapsed in late March. Both sides are still talking through back channels, but publicly both are escalating.

The Key Statements: What Trump Said and What It Means

Trump's "the shootin' starts" statement was made during a White House press briefing in early May 2026, where he described the potential US military response to a continued Hormuz closure as larger and more severe than prior strikes. He did not specify a deadline or define precisely what "bigger, better, and stronger" would entail operationally — a deliberate ambiguity that keeps Iran uncertain about timing and scale.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi responded within hours, calling the remarks "psychological warfare" and reaffirming that Iran would not reopen the strait under military threat. He added that Iran's conditions for reopening remain unchanged: an end to US military operations, sanctions relief, and recognition of Iran's nuclear rights. The US has rejected all three as preconditions.

The pattern here is recognizable. Trump used similar escalate-then-negotiate sequences during his first term with North Korea and in trade negotiations with China. The public threat serves to pressure Iran while keeping the Omani diplomatic channel active as a face-saving exit. Whether Iran reads it the same way — or whether hardliners use the threat to justify holding the position longer — is the central uncertainty.

The Strait of Hormuz: Why 21 Miles of Water Controls the Global Economy

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow chokepoint between Iran's southern coast and the Musandam Peninsula of Oman. At its narrowest the passable shipping lane is only about 21 miles wide, divided into two-mile inbound and outbound traffic lanes separated by a two-mile buffer zone. Every supertanker carrying oil from Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, or Qatar passes through this corridor.

According to the US Energy Information Administration, approximately 17 million barrels of crude oil, condensate, and refined petroleum products transited the strait daily in 2024. That represents roughly 21 percent of global petroleum liquids consumption. No alternative route can carry that volume — Saudi Arabia's east-west pipeline to the Red Sea port of Yanbu has been running at its maximum capacity of 7 million barrels per day since the closure, but that still leaves a 10 million barrel per day gap in global supply.

Japan, South Korea, India, and China — the four largest oil importers after the US — receive the vast majority of their crude through Hormuz. The closure has forced some tankers onto alternative routes adding weeks to delivery times and dramatically increasing shipping insurance costs. Lloyd's of London war risk premiums for Gulf transit have reached levels not seen since the Iran-Iraq War tanker conflict of the 1980s.

How We Got Here: The Background to the US-Iran Conflict

The current conflict has its immediate origins in the collapse of nuclear negotiations in late 2025 and early 2026. Iran's uranium enrichment had reached 60 percent purity — well above the 3.67 percent limit set by the 2015 JCPOA and technically capable of being further enriched to the 90 percent weapons-grade threshold within weeks. The IAEA reported in January 2026 that Iran's stockpile had grown large enough to produce fissile material for multiple nuclear weapons if the decision to enrich further was made.

US and Israeli intelligence assessments shared with Congress in February 2026 concluded that Iran was within weeks of a "breakout" decision. The Trump administration, facing pressure from Israel and its own national security establishment, authorized strikes on Iranian naval assets in the Persian Gulf and on missile production facilities on the Iranian mainland. Iran responded by announcing the Hormuz closure the following day.

The deeper structural conflict goes back further. The US withdrawal from the JCPOA in 2018, the reimposition of maximum pressure sanctions that cut Iran's oil exports by more than half, and eight years of accumulated mistrust on both sides created the conditions in which a diplomatic resolution became progressively harder to reach before the military phase began.

The Ceasefire Collapse: What Both Sides Are Saying

A ceasefire was announced through Oman on March 28, 2026. It lasted less than 72 hours before the US accused Iran of firing on a US surveillance drone over international waters. Iran denied the accusation and countered that the US had conducted a covert strike on an Iranian missile facility during the supposed ceasefire period. Neither side allowed independent verification of either claim.

European diplomats involved in the Omani channel described the collapse as consistent with a pattern seen in earlier attempted de-escalations: both sides agree to pause overt military action but continue what each considers "permitted" activities, leading to an incident that each side blames on the other. The result is that the ceasefire mechanism itself becomes discredited, making it harder to negotiate the next one.

Since the collapse, the US has deployed additional naval assets to the Persian Gulf, including additional destroyers and a second carrier strike group. The Pentagon stated these deployments are defensive in nature and intended to protect commercial shipping. Iran has characterized them as preparations for the expanded strike Trump has threatened.

Key Takeaways

  • Trump threatened a larger US military strike if Iran does not reopen the Strait of Hormuz — through which 17 million barrels of oil per day normally transit, representing 21 percent of global petroleum consumption.
  • The Hormuz closure has driven Brent crude to approximately $100 per barrel — a 52 percent increase from January 2026 levels — with Saudi Arabia's alternative pipeline running at maximum capacity but still unable to cover the supply gap.
  • A ceasefire brokered through Oman in late March 2026 collapsed within 72 hours, with both sides accusing the other of violations, leaving the diplomatic channel strained but technically still open.

How This Affects the Global Economy and Everyday Consumers

The economic impact of the Hormuz closure is being felt globally, not just in the oil markets. US gasoline prices have risen 35 to 45 cents per gallon depending on region. Airlines have imposed fuel surcharges of $15 to $50 per ticket on most routes. Industrial energy costs for manufacturers reliant on fuel oil and natural gas have risen significantly, contributing to the highest producer price inflation readings since 2022.

The International Monetary Fund has revised its 2026 global growth forecast downward by 0.8 percentage points since the conflict began, citing energy costs and trade disruption as the primary drivers. The World Bank has separately estimated that the supply shock, if it persists beyond six months, could push global economic growth below 2 percent for the year — a figure historically associated with recession conditions in advanced economies.

For everyday consumers the impact is most visible at the pump and in utility bills, but the second-order effects through food production costs, shipping of consumer goods, and industrial manufacturing are increasingly visible in retail prices across sectors. Airlines, shipping companies, and energy-intensive manufacturers are absorbing costs faster than they can pass them on.

What Experts Are Watching: The Scenarios Ahead

Defense analysts and energy economists are tracking three distinct scenarios. A negotiated limited deal through Oman — in which Iran reopens the strait in exchange for a suspension of new sanctions and a US pause in offensive operations — remains the most plausible near-term resolution and would produce an immediate partial recovery in oil prices. Saudi Aramco's CEO has said that even immediate reopening would take months to fully normalize the market, but prices would begin declining within days of a credible announcement.

A prolonged stalemate — the current trajectory — continues to strain both sides economically but carries the risk of miscalculation. Iran's economy, already under severe sanction pressure, is facing accelerating inflation and currency depreciation. US domestic political pressure from gasoline prices is rising as the summer driving season approaches. Either pressure point could push one side toward a decision — to escalate or to compromise — that changes the dynamic rapidly.

Military escalation remains the least likely but highest-impact scenario. A large-scale US strike aimed at degrading Iran's ability to enforce the closure — targeting mine-laying capabilities, naval missile batteries, and shore-based anti-ship systems — would likely produce an Iranian response targeting Gulf state oil infrastructure. That scenario would push oil prices above $130 per barrel and could tip the global economy into a recession that makes current economic pain look modest.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Strait of Hormuz and why is it critical?

The Strait of Hormuz is a 21-mile-wide shipping corridor between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the open ocean. Approximately 17 million barrels of oil per day transit it normally — about 21 percent of global petroleum liquids. No alternative route can carry that volume, making it the single most strategically critical energy chokepoint on Earth.

Why has Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz?

Iran closed the strait in late February 2026 following US military strikes on its naval and missile infrastructure. Tehran describes the closure as economic leverage to force the US back to negotiations. Iran's stated conditions for reopening include an end to US strikes, sanctions relief, and recognition of nuclear rights — all of which the US has declined as preconditions.

What has happened to oil prices since the strait was closed?

Brent crude rose from approximately $66 per barrel in January 2026 to around $100 per barrel by May 2026 — a roughly 52 percent increase. Saudi Arabia's east-west pipeline is running at maximum capacity of 7 million barrels per day as a partial bypass, but this cannot cover the 17 million barrels per day normally transiting the strait.

What exactly did Trump threaten regarding Iran?

Trump stated that if Iran does not reopen the strait, the US will launch a military response he described as bigger, better, and stronger than prior US strikes. He did not set a specific deadline. Diplomatic channels through Oman remain technically open alongside the public threat, suggesting the statement is intended as negotiating pressure rather than an imminent operational order.

Is the US-Iran ceasefire still holding?

No. A ceasefire announced through Oman on March 28, 2026 collapsed within 72 hours, with each side accusing the other of violations. Since then both sides have returned to active pressure — the US deploying additional naval assets, Iran maintaining the strait closure. Back-channel diplomacy continues through Oman but no new ceasefire framework has been announced.

Conclusion

Trump's "bigger, better, stronger" warning is the sharpest public escalation since the conflict began, but it comes in a context where both sides have strong incentives to avoid full-scale war while maintaining enough pressure to extract concessions. The Omani diplomatic channel remains the most likely path to any near-term resolution, and the question is whether Trump's public ultimatum accelerates Iran's calculation that the cost of holding the closure now exceeds the cost of negotiating an exit.

For the world's energy markets, consumers, and economies, the answer to that question matters enormously. Every week the strait stays closed is another week of $100 oil, rising inflation, and compounding economic damage. The diplomatic and military situation is moving faster than the economic data can track — and the outcome over the next few weeks will determine whether 2026 ends as a year of managed crisis or something considerably worse.
