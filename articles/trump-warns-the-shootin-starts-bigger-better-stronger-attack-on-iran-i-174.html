Trump Warns ‘The Shootin’ Starts’ – Bigger, Better & Stronger Attack on Iran if They Don’t Reopen Strait of Hormuz

By Sarah Mitchell Senior Tech Editor
Published: May 04, 2026 Updated: May 04, 2026 7 Min Read

A stark warning has been issued by the US, threatening severe consequences if Iran fails to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, a crucial waterway that connects the Persian Gulf to the Arabian Sea. This Strait is vital for international oil trade, with a significant portion of the world's oil passing through it. The situation is escalating, with the US and Iran engaging in a war of words, each side accusing the other of aggression.

The current situation matters right now because any disruption to the Strait of Hormuz could have far-reaching consequences for the global economy, particularly in the oil market. The threat of a stronger attack on Iran by the US raises concerns about regional stability and the potential for a wider conflict. As the situation continues to unfold, it is essential to understand the context and the potential implications of such a conflict.

What Was Said — The Key Statements

The US has explicitly stated that if Iran does not reopen the Strait of Hormuz, it will face a stronger attack. This statement is part of an escalating series of threats and counter-threats between the two nations. The language used by the US has been particularly strong, emphasizing the severity of the potential response.

The key statements from the US have focused on the importance of keeping the Strait of Hormuz open for international shipping, citing the need to maintain the free flow of oil and ensure global economic stability. Iran, on the other hand, has argued that it has the right to control its territorial waters and has accused the US of provocation.

The rhetoric from both sides has been intense, with each nation accusing the other of seeking to escalate the situation. The US has made it clear that it will not tolerate any disruption to the flow of oil through the Strait, while Iran has stated that it will defend its sovereignty and interests.

The Background — How We Got Here

The current tensions between the US and Iran have a long and complex history, with both nations having a fraught relationship that spans decades. The situation has been exacerbated by a series of incidents and disputes, including the US withdrawal from the Iran nuclear deal and the imposition of economic sanctions on Iran.

Iran has responded to these sanctions by taking steps to increase its uranium enrichment, which has led to concerns about its nuclear program. The US has also been critical of Iran's support for militant groups in the region and its human rights record. These tensions have created a volatile atmosphere, with both sides engaging in a series of provocations and counter-provocations.
