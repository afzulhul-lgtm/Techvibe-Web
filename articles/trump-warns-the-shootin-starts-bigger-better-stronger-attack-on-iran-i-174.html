The Strait of Hormuz, a vital shipping route connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman, has been a focal point of geopolitical tensions between the United States and Iran. The strait, which is approximately 90 miles wide at its narrowest point, is a critical passage for oil tankers and other ships, with estimates suggesting that around 20% of the world's oil supply passes through it. The current tensions have sparked concerns about the potential disruption of global oil supplies and the impact on the economy.

The United States and Iran have a complex and often contentious history, with relations deteriorating significantly in recent years. The US withdrawal from the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), also known as the Iran nuclear deal, in 2018 marked a significant turning point in the relationship between the two countries. Since then, the US has imposed stringent sanctions on Iran, targeting its oil exports, banking sector, and other key industries. Iran, in response, has taken steps to increase its uranium enrichment and has threatened to disrupt shipping in the Strait of Hormuz.

The US invasion of Iraq in 2003 had a significant impact on the region, leading to a power vacuum that Iran was able to exploit. The subsequent rise of Shia militias in Iraq and the growth of Iranian influence in the region have been major concerns for the US and its allies. The conflict in Syria, which began in 2011, has also seen the US and Iran on opposite sides, with the US supporting rebel groups and Iran backing the Assad regime.
