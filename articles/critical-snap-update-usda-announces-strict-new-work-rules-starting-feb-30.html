Critical SNAP Update: USDA Announces Strict New Work Rules Starting February 2026

By {{AUTHOR_NAME}} Senior Editor
Published: May 08, 2026 Updated: May 08, 2026 4 Min Read

As of February 2026, the United States Department of Agriculture has implemented significant changes to the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) work requirements, impacting millions of recipients nationwide. The updated regulations expand the categories of able-bodied adults without dependents who must work, train, or volunteer for at least 80 hours per month to maintain SNAP benefits eligibility.

The new rules mark a departure from the previous flexibility allowed under the Biden administration, where states could waive work requirements in areas with high unemployment. Now, states must demonstrate elevated unemployment rates in specific county-level labor market areas, rather than relying on statewide metrics, to justify waivers. This shift is expected to affect recipients between the ages of 18 and 55 without children under 18 in the household.

What the New USDA SNAP Work Requirements Say

The expanded work requirements mandate that eligible recipients engage in acceptable activities, such as paid employment, approved job training programs, community service, or enrollment in educational programs, for a minimum of 80 hours per month. Recipients must document their qualifying activities through their state SNAP agency's reporting portal to maintain benefits eligibility.

Exemptions remain in place for individuals with documented disabilities, pregnant women, primary caregivers of children under six, and those determined by state agencies to be medically unable to work. The USDA has announced additional funding for state agencies to support compliance assistance and outreach efforts.

Who Is Affected by the February 2026 Changes

Approximately 2.5 million able-bodied adults without dependents, aged 18-55, are expected to be impacted by the new work requirements. These individuals, without children under 18 in the household, will need to meet the 80-hour monthly threshold to maintain SNAP benefits. The changes aim to encourage recipients to engage in productive activities, while also providing support for those who genuinely require assistance.

How to Comply with the New Work Rules

To comply with the new regulations, recipients must report their qualifying activities through their state SNAP agency's portal. Acceptable activities include paid employment, job training programs, community service, and educational enrollment. Recipients can contact their local SNAP agency for guidance on reporting requirements and available resources.

  • 80 hours of paid employment per month
  • 80 hours of approved job training programs per month
  • 80 hours of community service per month
  • 80 hours of educational enrollment per month

Key Takeaways

  • New SNAP work requirements took effect in February 2026
  • Able-bodied adults without dependents must work, train, or volunteer for 80 hours per month
  • Exemptions remain in place for individuals with disabilities, pregnant women, and primary caregivers of young children

Conclusion

The new SNAP work requirements, effective February 2026, aim to encourage able-bodied adults without dependents to engage in productive activities while maintaining support for those who genuinely require assistance. With the expanded categories of eligible recipients and stricter waiver authority, states must adapt to the changes and provide necessary guidance to recipients.

As the USDA provides additional funding for state agencies to support compliance assistance and outreach efforts, it is essential for recipients to understand the new requirements and available resources. By reporting qualifying activities and meeting the 80-hour monthly threshold, eligible recipients can maintain SNAP benefits eligibility and access essential nutrition assistance.
