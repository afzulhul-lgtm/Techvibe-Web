Long Before Trees Earth Was Ruled by This Mysterious Giant Lifeform

By {{AUTHOR_NAME}} Senior Editor
Published: May 08, 2026 Updated: May 08, 2026 7 Min Read

Imagine a world where the tallest organism on land was not a tree, but a giant fungus that stood up to 8 meters tall, roughly the height of a two-story building, and up to 1 meter in diameter. This was the world of the late Silurian and Devonian periods, roughly 420 to 350 million years ago. The discovery of this mysterious giant lifeform, known as Prototaxites, has significant implications for our understanding of ancient terrestrial ecosystems and the evolution of life on Earth.

The discovery of Prototaxites matters now because it challenges our previous understanding of the early history of life on Earth. For decades, scientists believed that the first trees were the dominant organisms on land, but the discovery of Prototaxites reveals a more complex and fascinating story. The fact that a giant fungus was able to thrive in this environment raises important questions about the role of fungi in shaping the evolution of life on Earth.

What Scientists Discovered About Prototaxites

Prototaxites was first discovered over 150 years ago, but its true nature has only recently been uncovered through modern isotopic analysis of carbon ratios within fossilized specimens. The carbon-13 to carbon-12 ratios found in specimens closely match the isotopic signatures of organisms that fed on decomposing organic matter, consistent with fungal biology rather than photosynthesis-based plant life. This finding is significant because it reveals that Prototaxites was almost certainly a giant fungus, the largest organism ever to live on land at that time.

The discovery of Prototaxites has been a collaborative effort, with research teams from the University of Chicago and the Smithsonian Institution contributing key analyses. The team's findings have provided compelling evidence that Prototaxites was a giant fungus, and have fundamentally reshaped our picture of ancient terrestrial ecosystems. Rather than barren landscapes punctuated only by primitive plants, the pre-forest Earth may have been dotted with these enormous fungal towers, acting as decomposers and nutrient cyclers in a world just beginning to colonize the land.
